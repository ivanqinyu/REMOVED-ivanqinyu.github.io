Graph I: Euler walks

DEFINITION:

(graph): A graph is a pair G = (V, E), where V = V(G) is a set of vertices and E = E(G) is a set of edges contained in { {u,v} : u,v ∈V, u ≠ v}

(walk): A walk in graph G = (V, E) is a sequence of vertices v~0~,v~1~, … ,v~k~ such that {v~i~, v~i+1~}∈E for all i ≤ k-1.

(form to; length): We say the walk is from v~0~ to v~k~ and call k the length of the walk.

(Euler walk): An Euler walk is one which contains every edge in G exactly once.

(equal): Two graphs G~1~ = (V~1~, E~1~) and G~2~ = (V~2~, E~2~) are equal, written G~1~ = G~2~,
if V~1~ = V~2~ and E~1~ = E~2~.

(isomorphic): G~1~ and G~2~ are isomorphic, written G~1~ ≌ G~2~, if there’s a bijection
f : V~1~ → V~2~ such that {f (u), f (v)} ∈ E~2~ if and only if {u, v} ∈ E~1~.

(neighbourhood): In a graph G = (V, E), the neighbourhood of a vertex v is the set of vertices joined to v by an edge.

Formally: N~G~ (v) = {w ∈ V : {v,w} ∈ E}.

Also, for all sets of vertices X ⊆ V, we write N~G~(X) = ∪~v∈X~ N~G~ (v).

(degree): The degree of a vertex v is the number of vertices joined to v. Formally:
d~G~ (v) = |N~G~ (v)|.

Theorem of Euler walk: If G has an Euler walk, then either:

  • every vertex of G has even degree; or
  • all but two vertices v~0~ and v~k~ have even degree, and any Euler walk
    must have v~0~ and v~k~ as endpoints.

proof of “if”: any walk with v~0~ = v~k~ uses an even number of edges from
every vertex, since it leaves each vertex immediately after entering.
Similarly, any walk with v~0~≠v~k~ uses an odd number of edges from v~0~ and
v~k~ , and an even number from any other vertex.

proof of “only if” (case (i)): Suppose G is connected, v ∈ V, and every vertex of G has even degree.

Form a walk W = w~0~ . . .w~k~ as follows:

  • take w~0~ = v;
  • take w~i~ to be an arbitrary neighbour of w~i−1~ such that {w~i−1~,w~i~} is not already a W-edge;
  • stop when N(w~i~) ⊆ {w~0~, . . . ,w~i−1~}

This need not give an Euler walk…
But it still gives us something.
Claim: w~k~ = w~0~ = v.
Proof: For a vertex x, how many W-edges are incident to x?

  • Two for each time x appears in {w~1~, . . . ,w~k−1~};
  • Plus one if x = w~0~;
  • Plus one if x = w~k~ .
    We know w~k~ has d(w~k~) W-edges, and d(w~k~ ) is even, so w~0~ = w~k~ .

Idea: Strong induction on |E|.
Base case: |E| = 0, immediate.
Induction step: Apply induction hypothesis to find Euler walks W~i~ for all non-empty components C~i~ of the subgraph G − W formed by removing W ’s edges from G:
Each vertex has even degree in both W and G, and hence also in G − W .
Each component C~i~ is connected by definition.
Idea: “Walk along W until we hit C~1~, then follow W~1~, then go back to W , and so on.”
G connected ⇒ there is a path P~i~ from v to each C~i~. Let v~i~ be the first vertex in C~i~ on P~i~.
Then some edge incident to vi must have been removed in G − W , or the vertex before v~i~ on P~i~ would have been part of C~i~.
So v~i~ ∈W.
We have found vertices v~i~ which lie in both C~i~ and W .
Wlog, if G has r non-trivial components, W reaches first v~1~, then v~2~, and so on up to v~r~. (Otherwise we can just reorder C~1~, . . . , C~r~.)
So our idea works! We follow W until reaching v1, then follow all of W1 (returning to v1), then follow W until reaching v2, and so on.

proof of “only if”: Suppose u ≠ v, d(u) and d(v) are odd, and every other degree is even.
Form a new graph, G’, by adding a new vertex w and edges from u to w
and w to v. Then every vertex of G’ has even degree, so G’ contains an
Euler walk W starting and ending at u by part (i).
Then we remove the subpath uwv from W , which turns it into an Euler
walk from u to v in G.

Does every graph satisfying (i) or (ii) have an Euler walk?
No, the graph doesn’t contain a Euler walk if the graph is not connected.

(path): path is a walk in which no vertices repeat.

(connected): A graph is connected if any two vertices are joined by a path.

Let G = (V, E) be a graph.
(subgraph): A subgraph H = (V~H~, E~H~) of G is a graph with V~H~ ⊆ V and E~H~ ⊆ E.
(induced subgraph): H is an induced subgraph if V~H~ ⊆ V and E~H~ = {e ∈ E : e ⊆ V~H~}.
(induced): For all vertex sets X ⊆ V, the graph induced by X is G[X] = (X, {e ∈ E : e ⊆ X}).

(component): A component H of G is a maximal connected induced subgraph of G.

Graph II: Directed graph and Hamilton cycle

(digraph): A directed graph (or digraph) is a pair G = (V, E), where V is a set of vertices and E is a set of edges contained in {(u, v) : u, v ∈ V, u ≠ v} }. Notice: Here in diagraph is “(” , “)”

(walk in digraph): A walk in a digraph G is defined in (almost) the same way as in a graph: a sequence of vertices w~0~ . . .w~k~ with (w~i~,w~i+1~) ∈ E for all i ≤ k − 1.

(path in diagraph): A path is a walk with no repeated vertices.

(Euler walk in diagraph): an Euler walk is a walk which uses every edge in the graph exactly once.

(strongly connected): G is strongly connected if for all u, v ∈ V, there is a path from u to v and from v to u.

(weakly connected): G is weakly connected if for all u, v ∈ V, there is a path from u to v or from v to u.

(strong/weak components): As with graphs, strong components (resp. weak components) of digraphs are maximal strongly (resp. weakly) connected subgraphs.

A digraph definitely can’t have an Euler walk if it’s not weakly connected!
And it can’t have one with equal endpoints if it’s not strongly connected.

(neighbourhood in diagraph): As with undirected graphs, the neighbourhood N(v) of a vertex v is the set of edges it’s adjacent to,

(degree in diagraph): its degree d(v) is the number of edges it’s in.

(in/out-neighbourhood): We define the in-neighbourhood N~−(v)~ and the out-neighbourhood N~+(v)~ of a vertex v by

N~−(v)~ = {(u, v) ∈ E(G): u ∈ V(G)},
N~+(v)~ = {(v,w) ∈ E(G): w ∈ V(G)}.

(in/out degree): The in-degree d~−(v)~ is |N~−(v)~|, and the out-degree d~+(v)~ is |N~+(v)~|.
Note that d(v) = d~−(v)~ + d~+(v)~.

We have shown that if W is an Euler walk, for any vertex x, either:

  • x = w~0~ = w~k~ and d^+^(x) = d^−^(x); or

  • x ∉ {w0, wk} and d^+^(x) = d^−^(x); or

  • x = w~0~ ≠ w~k~ and d^+^(x) = d^−^(x) + 1; or

  • x = w~k~ ≠ w~0~ and d^−^(x) = d^+^(x) + 1.

  • d^+^(v) = d^−^(v) for all v ∈ V and w~0~ = w~k~ ; or

  • d^−^(v) = d^+^(v) for all v ∉ {w~0~, w~k~}, d^+^(w~0~) = d^−^(w~0~) + 1, and d^−^(w~k~) = d^+^(w~k~) + 1. (So also w~0~ ≠ w~k~.)

(cycle): A cycle is a walk W = w~0~ . . .w~k~ with w~0~ = w~k~ and k ≥ 3, in which every vertex appears at most once except for w~0~ and w~k~ (which appear twice).
(Hamilton cycle): A Hamilton cycle is a cycle containing every vertex in the graph.

(Dirac’s Theorem): Any n-vertex graph G with minimum degree at least n/2 has a Hamilton cycle.

Proof: Try to find a long path inductively: start with a trivial one-vertex path and repeatedly extend it.
So suppose G contains a k-vertex path v~1~ . . . v~k~ for some k ∈ [n − 1].
Case 1: k ≤ n/2. Then our usual trick of greedy extension works!
We have d(v~k~) ≥ n/2 > |{v~1~, . . . , v~k−1~}|.
So there’s a vertex v~k+1~ adjacent to v~k~ other than v~1~, . . . , v~k−1~.
Then v~1~ . . . v~k+1~ is a path of length k + 1.
Case 2: k > n/2. Suppose G contains a k-vertex path v~1~ . . . v~k~ . Greedy extension may not work… but try anyway!
Case 2a: There exists a vertex v~k+1~ ∈ N(v~k~ ) \ {v~1~, . . . , v~k−1~}.
Then v~1~ . . . v~k+1~ is a (k + 1)-vertex path.
Case 2b: There exists a vertex v~0~ ∈ N(v~k~) \ {v~2~, . . . , v~k~}.
Then v~0~ . . . v~k~ is a (k + 1)-vertex path.
Case 2c: Both N(v~1~) ⊆ {v~2~, . . . , v~k~} and N(v~k~ ) ⊆ {v~1~, . . . , v~k−1~}.
In this case, we use the fact that greedy extension fails to extend the path in another way.
In general: We seek v~i~ ∈ N(v~1~) such that v~i−1~ ∈ N(v~k~).
Then v~1~v~i~. . . v~k~ v~i−1~v~i−2~ . . . v~1~ will be a cycle on {v~1~, . . . , v~k~ }.
There are at least n/2 vertices v~i~ ∈ N(v~1~), hence at least n/2 vertices in {v~i−1~ : v~i~ ∈ N(v~1~)}. There are also at least n/2 vertices in N(v~k~).
Both sets are contained in {v~1~, . . . , v~k−1~}, which has size at most n − 1.
So they must intersect. This works even when k = n.
So suppose G has a cycle v~1~ . . . v~k~ , with n/2 < k < n, and let v~k+1~ be an arbitrary vertex not in the cycle.
We have d(v~k+1~) ≥ n/2, and |{v~1~, . . . , v~k~ }| > n/2, and the graph has n vertices. So v~k+1~ must be adjacent to some v~i~ on the cycle.
Then v~k+1~v~i~. . . v~k~ v~1~ . . . v~i−1~ is a (k + 1)-vertex path.

Graph III: Trees and miscellany

DEFINITION:

(The Handshake Lemma): For any graph G = (V, E), Σ~v∈V~ d(v) = 2|E|.

(Directed Handshake Lemma): For any digraph G = (V, E), Σ~v∈V~d^+^(v) = Σ~v∈V~d^−^(v) = |E|.

Proof: All edges contain two vertices, and each vertex v is in d(v) edges. The idea of this proof is called double-counting.

(k-regular): A graph is k-regular if every vertex has degree k (so cycles are 2-regular).

(forest and tree): We define a forest to be a graph which contains no cycles, and a tree to be a connected graph with no cycles.

(So the components of a forest are trees, and all trees are forests!)

(A tree is a connected graph with no cycles)

Lemma1: If T = (V, E) is a tree, then any pair of vertices u, v ∈ V is joined by a unique path uTv in T.

Proof: T is connected, so there is a path P~1~ = x~0~ . . . x~k~ from u to v.
Suppose there is another path P~2~ = y~0~ . . . y~k~ from u to v.
Then P~1~ and P~2~ must diverge from each other and come back together.
Let I = min{i : x~i~ ≠ y~i~} be the point of divergence.
Let J = min{i > I : x~i~ ∈ {y~i~, . . . , y~k~ } } be the point of remerging.
Let K be the corresponding point on P~2~, so y~K~ = x~J~ .
Then x~I−1~x~I~. . . x~J~ y~K−1~y~K−2~ . . . y~I−1~ is a cycle, so T is not a tree.

Lemma 2: Any n-vertex tree has n − 1 edges

Proof: We start by showing how to turn a tree T = (V, E) into a rooted tree,
let r ∈ V be arbitrary — this will be the root.
Every vertex v ≠ r has a unique path Pv from r to v by the lemma.
Direct its edges from r to v.
Because these paths are unique, every vertex other than r has in-degree 1, and r has in-degree 0.
So by the directed handshake lemma: |E| =Σ~v∈V~d^−^(v) = n − 1.

In a rooted tree with root r:
(ancestor/descendant): u is an ancestor of v (or v is a descendant) of u) if u is on P~v~.
(parent/child): u is the parent of v (or v is a child of u) if u ∈ N^-^(v).
(level): The first level L~0~ of T is {r}, and L~i+1~ = N^+^(L~i~).
(depth): The depth of T is the number of non-empty levels
(leaf): In any tree: a leaf is a degree-1 vertex.

Lemma 3: Any n-vertex tree T = (V, E) with n ≥ 2 has at least 2 leaves.

Proof: Let x be the number of leaves in T…
By the handshaking lemma, |E| =1/2 * Σ~v∈V~d(v). Also, |E| = n − 1.
Since T is connected and n ≥ 2, every vertex has degree at least 1.
So all non-leaves have degree at least 2, and Σ~v∈V~d(v) ≥ 2(n − x) + x.
Plugging this in gives |E| = n − 1 = 1/2 Σ~v∈V~d(v) ≥ n −x/2.
Solving for x gives x ≥ 2, so we’re done!

Lemma: The following are equivalent for an n-vertex graph T = (V, E):
(A) T is connected and has no cycles, i.e. is a tree;
(B) T has n − 1 edges and is connected;
© T has n − 1 edges and has no cycles;
(D) T has a unique path between any pair of vertices.

We’ve already proved (A) ⇒ (D) (Lemma 1) as well as (A) ⇒ (B) and (A) ⇒© (Lemma 2).

(D)⇒(A):

T has a path between any pair of vertices, so it’s connected.
And on any cycle v~0~ . . . v~k~ , there are two different paths from v~0~ to v~k~ :

  • the path v~0~ . . . v~k~ ; and
  • the edge v~0~v~k~.

So T has no cycles.

©⇒(A):

Suppose T has no cycles and components C~1~, . . . , C~r~.
Each of these components has no cycles, and is connected, so it’s a tree.
So by (A) ⇒ (B) (or Lemma 2), each C~i~ has |V(C~i~)| − 1 edges.
Every edge of T is in some C~i~, so |E| =Σ~i~(|V(C~i~)| − 1) = n − r.
But we know |E| = n − 1, so we must have r = 1.

(B)⇒(A):

We claim that if T = (V, E) is connected, and e ∈ E is on a cycle, then T − e is connected.
If e is not in P: Let a, b ∈ V. T is connected, so there’s a path P = x~0~ . . . x~k~ from a to b.
If P doesn’t contain e, P is also a path in T − e and we’re done.

If e is in P: Write e = {x~l~, x~l+1~}. Let C = y~0~ . . . y~l~ be a cycle in T containing e — without loss of generality we can take y~0~ = x~l~ and y~l~ = x~l+1~.

We form a new graph T’ by repeatedly removing edges from cycles in T (in arbitrary order) until no more cycles remain.
Then T’ has no cycles, and the Claim implies it’s connected, so it’s a tree.
So by (A) ⇒ (B) (or Lemma 2), T’ has n − 1 edges.
But T also has n − 1 edges! So we must have T’ = T, and T never had any cycles to begin with.

DEFINITION

(matching): A matching in a graph is a collection of disjoint edges.

(perfect): It is perfect if every vertex is contained in some matching edge.

(bipartite/bipartition): A graph G = (V, E) is bipartite if V can be partitioned into disjoint sets A and B which contain no edges; these are a bipartition.
